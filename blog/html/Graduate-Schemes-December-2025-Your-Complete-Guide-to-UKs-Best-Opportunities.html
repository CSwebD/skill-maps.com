Why December Matters for Graduate Schemes

As someone actively navigating the graduate job market in London, I can tell you that December is absolutely crucial for your graduate scheme applications. Whilst many students are winding down for Christmas, the smartest candidates are getting their applications in before the new year rush.

Here's what most people don't realise: approximately seventy per cent of graduate scheme positions for September 2026 start dates will be filled by Christmas. The companies that haven't closed applications by December are often the ones operating on rolling admissions—meaning they review and offer places as applications come in, not after a deadline.

The maths is simple but stark. Final-year students who submit applications by October receive twenty-five per cent more interview invitations than those who wait until January. By the time January arrives, many schemes have already conducted first-round interviews and are extending offers to their top candidates.

This guide isn't about overwhelming you with every single graduate scheme out there. Instead, I've compiled practical insights from my own application experience, combined with research on what actually works in 2025. Whether you're targeting technology, finance, engineering, law, or something completely different, understanding how graduate schemes work—and how to stand out—can make all the difference.

What Actually Are Graduate Schemes?

The Basics

A graduate scheme is essentially a structured training programme designed specifically for recent university graduates. Unlike standard graduate jobs where you're hired for one specific role, graduate schemes typically last between one and three years and often include rotations through different departments.

Think of it as a professional apprenticeship for graduates. You're paid a full salary—usually quite competitive—whilst receiving formal training, mentorship, and exposure to multiple areas of the business. The goal is to fast-track talented individuals into more senior positions by giving them a comprehensive understanding of how the organisation operates.

Why Companies Run Graduate Schemes

From the employer's perspective, graduate schemes are talent pipelines. They're investing in developing future leaders who understand their company culture, systems, and strategic priorities. It's far more cost-effective to train someone from scratch than to hire experienced professionals at senior levels who might not fit the organisational culture.

For you as a graduate, this means opportunities at organisations you might not otherwise access. Top firms like Deloitte, the NHS, Goldman Sachs, and the Civil Service actively seek out bright graduates who show potential rather than requiring years of experience. It's one of the few times in your career where your degree classification and potential matter more than your CV.

The Reality Check

Here's what they don't always tell you: graduate schemes are brutally competitive. We're talking about acceptance rates that would make Oxbridge blush. Some schemes receive over a hundred applications for every position. The selection process can involve multiple stages—online applications, psychometric tests, video interviews, assessment centres, and final interviews—spread across several months.

But don't let that discourage you. The competition is stiff precisely because these programmes offer genuine value. The average graduate scheme salary starts at around £29,000 to £32,000, but can reach £47,000 for specialist programmes. More importantly, research shows that eighty-three per cent of graduates receive salary increases after completing their schemes, with average salaries rising from £32,000 to £50,000 over three years—a fifty-six per cent increase.

The Application Process: What to Expect

Stage 1: Online Application

This is where most candidates fall at the first hurdle. The application form isn't just about filling in your details—it's your first interview. Expect to answer competency-based questions, sometimes requiring 250-300 words each. Questions like "Describe a time when you demonstrated leadership" or "Why do you want to work for our company?" aren't casual—they're being scored against specific criteria.

Top tip from experience: Don't use generic answers. Every single application should be tailored to that specific company and programme. Reference their values, recent news, specific aspects of their scheme that appeal to you. Recruiters can spot copy-paste answers a mile away, and they're an instant rejection.

Stage 2: Psychometric Testing

Around eighty-four per cent of UK employers use online aptitude tests in their graduate recruitment process. These typically include numerical reasoning (interpreting data and graphs), verbal reasoning (analysing written information), and logical reasoning (pattern recognition). Some also include situational judgement tests where you respond to workplace scenarios.

Here's what helped me: practice beforehand. Genuinely, practice makes a massive difference. Sites like AssessmentDay, JobTestPrep, and GraduateFirst offer test simulations. Familiarity with question formats significantly improves both speed and accuracy. The tests are usually timed, so knowing what to expect reduces anxiety and helps you perform better.

Stage 3: Video Interviews

Video interviews have become standard since 2020. These can be either pre-recorded (you record responses to questions with limited preparation time) or live with a recruiter. For pre-recorded interviews, you'll typically get 30 seconds to read a question and 2-3 minutes to respond.

Practical advice: Set up in a quiet, well-lit space. Test your technology beforehand. Look at the camera, not the screen. Dress professionally from head to toe (yes, even though they can only see your top half—it affects your mindset). And please, remove anything embarrassing from your background. One candidate got rejected because their shelf had a collection of empty beer bottles visible behind them.

Stage 4: Assessment Centre

If you've made it this far, congratulations—you're in the top ten to twenty per cent of applicants. Assessment centres typically last a full day and include multiple activities: group exercises, presentations, case studies, individual interviews, and sometimes written tasks.

The group exercise is where many people slip up. You're being assessed on teamwork, communication, and leadership—not on having the loudest voice or the "best" answer. Listen actively, build on others' ideas, facilitate discussion, and be aware of time. The candidates who dominate the conversation rarely get offers. The ones who bring the whole team to a strong conclusion do.

Stage 5: Final Interview

The final interview is usually with senior managers or partners. Expect in-depth competency questions using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result). They'll probe your examples, asking follow-up questions to test whether you're genuinely describing your experiences or embellishing.

Prepare at least five to seven strong examples from your university projects, part-time work, volunteering, or extracurricular activities. Make sure they cover different competencies: leadership, teamwork, problem-solving, resilience, communication, and commercial awareness. Quantify your results wherever possible—"increased social media engagement by 40%" is much stronger than "improved our social media presence."

How to Actually Stand Out

Apply Early—Seriously

Many top schemes operate on rolling admissions. This means they review applications as they come in and can close when they've found enough quality candidates—sometimes weeks before the official deadline. Aim to submit within the first two to three weeks of applications opening.

I made this mistake in my first round of applications. I waited until mid-November thinking I had plenty of time until the January deadline. By the time I submitted, several firms had already filled most of their spots and were only occasionally inviting exceptional candidates. Don't repeat my error.

Research Thoroughly

Generic applications are death. Every company can tell when you've copy-pasted answers. Before applying, spend genuine time understanding the company. Read their annual reports, recent news articles, follow them on LinkedIn, watch employee testimonials on YouTube. Understand their values, culture, recent projects, and strategic priorities.

Reference specific things in your application. "I'm impressed by your AI for Good initiative" beats "I admire your commitment to innovation" every single time. Specificity demonstrates genuine interest.

Quality Over Quantity

It's tempting to apply to fifty schemes and hope something sticks. Resist this urge. Five really strong, tailored applications will yield better results than twenty rushed ones. Each application should feel personalised to that specific company and role.

I've applied to both extremes. In my first attempt, I sent out 30 applications with minor tweaks. Got three interviews, zero offers. Second attempt, I focused on 12 carefully chosen schemes with fully customised applications. Got seven interviews, two offers. The difference was night and day.

Use Your Network

Attend virtual and in-person careers fairs. Connect with current graduate scheme participants on LinkedIn and politely ask for 15-minute informal chats. Most people are surprisingly willing to help if you're respectful and specific about what you'd like to learn.

Follow companies on LinkedIn and Twitter. They often share application tips, what they're looking for, and insights into their assessment process. The NHS Graduate Management Training Scheme, for instance, regularly posts tips from current trainees about preparing for assessment centres.

If You Have a 2:2

Many graduate schemes require a 2:1 degree minimum, but not all. Companies like BBC, some Civil Service streams, Lidl, and various smaller firms focus more on aptitude and fit than degree classification. Some have even started running internal assessments rather than requiring CVs, opening opportunities for less academic but highly capable candidates.

If you have a 2:2, compensate with strong work experience, impressive extracurriculars, clear evidence of skills development, and stellar application answers. Also, consider schemes that explicitly welcome 2:2 applicants rather than wasting time on those with rigid academic requirements.

Show Personality

Graduate schemes aren't just about qualifications—they're about finding people who'll fit into the team and represent the company well. Let your personality come through in applications and interviews. Be professional, yes, but also be human.

I've seen candidates lose offers because they were too robotic in interviews. Companies want people who are engaging, authentic, and genuinely enthusiastic. If you're excited about the opportunity, show it. If you're nervous, it's okay to acknowledge that too. Authenticity resonates.

Final Thoughts: Is It Worth It?

Look, I won't pretend graduate schemes are perfect. The application process is exhausting. The competition is fierce. You'll face rejection—probably multiple times. Some schemes have unrealistic workloads or don't live up to their promises. And there's always a risk that you'll commit to two years only to discover it's not what you wanted.

But here's what I've learned from both my applications and from speaking with people currently on schemes: for most graduates, they're worth considering. The structured training, the exposure to different business areas, the mentorship, the networking opportunities, and yes, the competitive salary and progression prospects—these things matter, especially when you're just starting out.

Graduate schemes aren't the only path. Plenty of successful professionals never went through formal programmes. If you're entrepreneurial, have a specific passion, want to dive straight into a particular role, or simply don't want the structure, that's completely valid. But if you're uncertain about your direction, want comprehensive training, value career progression frameworks, and are willing to work hard in a competitive environment, they're genuinely valuable.

The key is being strategic. Don't apply to every scheme you see. Research thoroughly, understand what you're committing to, tailor every application, and be genuinely enthusiastic about the opportunities you pursue. Quality matters far more than quantity.

And remember—December isn't your last chance. If you miss these deadlines, there will be January openings, spring recruitment, and rolling opportunities throughout the year. But if you're ready now, don't wait. The best time to apply was October. The second-best time is today.

Good luck with your applications. You've got this. And if you face rejection? Learn from it, improve, and keep going. The right opportunity will come—often from the application you least expected.
